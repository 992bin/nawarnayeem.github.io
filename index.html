About Me

I am a quantitative researcher in health economics and psychiatric epidemiology, specializing in the application of microeconometrics and machine learning to large-scale health data. My work focuses on understanding risk factors and policy impacts related to mental health outcomes, including suicidality, ADHD, and substance use.

I earned my PhD in Economics from Lehigh University under the supervision of Dr. Shin-Yi Chou, and previously served as a Postdoctoral Research Fellow in the Department of Psychiatry and Behavioral Neuroscience under the mentorship of Dr. Daniel Lin at Saint Louis University. I have worked closely with interdisciplinary teams of clinicians, epidemiologists, and health economists across multiple institutions, including the University of Pennsylvania, University of New South Wales (UNSW), SUNY Buffalo, SUNY Upstate Medical University, Michigan State University, the University of Louisville, the University of Vermont, Saint Louis University, and Lehigh University.

Research Interests

Health economics, psychiatric epidemiology, causal inference, machine learning, substance use, suicidality, mental health services research, and health disparities.

Teaching

I have taught Health Economics at both the Ph.D. and undergraduate levels, as well as Principles of Microeconomics, at Louisiana State University. I also taught business statistics to MBA students at Lehigh University and delivered lectures on causal inference and applied quantitative methods in epidemiology at Saint Louis University School of Medicine.
